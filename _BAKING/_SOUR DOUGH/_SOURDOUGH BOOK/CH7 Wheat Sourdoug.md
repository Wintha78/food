

### 1. Preparing the Starter and Ingredients

- **Starter Readiness:** A successful loaf begins with an active starter. If it has been sitting in the fridge, the author recommends **refreshing it one to three times** (using ratios like 1:5:5 or 1:10:10) to ensure the yeast is active enough to outcompete bacteria and provide sufficient lift.
- **The Mix:** The basic recipe consists of bread flour, water, salt, and starter. Adding **10–20% whole wheat flour** is recommended because its higher enzymatic activity speeds up the fermentation process.
- **Hydration:** While high hydration (more water) can lead to an airier crumb, it makes the dough difficult to handle. For beginners, the sources recommend a **hydration level of around 60–70%**.

### 2. Development (Fermentolysis and Dough Strength)

- **[[Fermentolysis]]:** The author advocates for mixing all ingredients (including the starter and salt) at once rather than using a traditional "autolyse" (mixing only flour and water first). This simplifies the process and allows the enzymes time to break down the flour while the microbes begin working.
- **Creating Strength:** Kneading builds the gluten network needed to trap CO2. A key insight from the sources is that **waiting is part of kneading**—simply letting the dough rest for 15 minutes allows gluten bonds to form automatically.
- **Rounding:** After kneading, you must create a **smooth dough ball**. By dragging the dough over a surface to tighten the outer gluten layer, you reduce its stickiness and make it easier to handle during later stages.

### 3. Bulk Fermentation

This is the most challenging stage, where the dough undergoes its primary rise.

- **Monitoring:** The author warns against following recipes' timing blindly, as temperature and starter activity vary. Instead, use an **Aliquot jar**—a small sample of dough in a cylindrical container—to monitor for a specific size increase (typically 25% to 100% depending on the flour's protein).
- **Stretch and Folds:** During this stage, you periodically stretch and fold the dough to strengthen the gluten and redistribute heat and gases, which leads to a more even crumb structure.

### 4. Shaping and Proofing

- **Dividing and Preshaping:** If making multiple loaves, divide the dough and round each piece to add strength and ensure uniform shapes.
- **Final Shaping:** The source details a **batard (oblong) shaping technique**, which involves folding a rectangular piece of dough into a tight roll and sealing the edges.
- **Proofing:** The final rise can happen at **room temperature** (tested using the **finger poke test**, where a dent should stay visible for one minute) or in the **fridge (retarding)**. Cold-proofing makes the dough easier to handle and score.

### 5. Scoring

Just before baking, use a sharp razor blade to make an incision in the dough at a **45° angle**. This controls where the bread expands and helps create an **"ear"**—a thin, crisp, dark edge that enhances both the look and the flavor of the final loaf.
