**Fermentolysis** is a bread-making technique that combines the benefits of **autolyse** and **fermentation** into a single, simplified step. While a traditional autolyse involves mixing only flour and water and letting them rest before adding the starter and salt, fermentolysis involves **mixing all ingredients together at once**, including a small amount of sourdough starter.

Key aspects of the fermentolysis process include:

- **Simplification of Workflow:** The primary advantage is that it eliminates the need to mix the dough multiple times. In a standard autolyse, incorporating salt and starter into a pre-hydrated dough can be cumbersome and feel like kneading from scratch again.
- **Low Inoculation Rate:** To make fermentolysis effective, the author recommends using a **small amount of starter**, typically around **5% to 10%**. This slows down the rate of fermentation, allowing the microbes to replicate gradually while giving enzymes sufficient time to work.
- **Enzymatic Activity:** By extending the overall fermentation time (for example, opting for a single 7-hour fermentation instead of a 2-hour autolyse followed by 5 hours of bulk fermentation), the **amylase and protease enzymes** have enough time to break down the flour. This improves the dough's **extensibility**, makes it easier to handle, and enhances the final bread's flavor and crust browning.
- **Adaptability:** This method allows the baker to adjust for environmental factors. On warm days, using even less starter (as low as 1%) can slow the process down enough to maintain the desired 8 to 12-hour fermentation window, ensuring the enzymes have time to do their job without the dough over-fermenting.

## The Order for Traditional Autolyse

If you choose to do a traditional autolyse, you do not add any starter at the beginning.

- **Step 1:** Mix **only flour and water**.
- **Step 2:** Let the mixture rest (typically 30 minutes to several hours).
- **Step 3:** Add the **entire amount of starter and the salt** at the same time after the rest period is over.

## The Order for Fermentolysis (Recommended)

The author advocates for fermentolysis because adding starter and salt to an already hydrated dough can be difficult and feel like kneading from scratch.

- **The Order:** You **mix all the primary ingredients at once**—flour, water, salt, and the sourdough starter.
- **The Technique:** Instead of splitting the starter into two additions, you simply use a **smaller total amount of starter** (typically 5% to 10%). This small amount slows down the fermentation enough to allow the enzymes to work on the flour, effectively combining the "autolyse" and "fermentation" phases into one single step.
