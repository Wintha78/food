

Below I explain, ingredient by ingredient, what salt does (flavor + chemistry + practical salting advice). I’m using the principles from [[Salt]] and the heat/protein guidance in [[Heat]].

 **Chicken (breast)**
- - What salt does: seasons through the meat, ==causes some protein to solubilize into a gel that holds more water (juiciness), and makes the chicken taste “more== chickeny.”
- - Practical: dry-brine ~12–24 hours ahead for best penetration and moisture retention. Use kosher salt and pat skin dry before roasting/searing to allow browning.

 **Chicken (thighs/legs)**
- - What salt does: seasons, ==helps collagen-containing areas retain moisture and break down more evenly during cooking, and improves flavor throughout.==
- - Practical: can be salted earlier (hours to a day). Because these cuts tolerate higher temp, you can salt closer to cooking if you want crisp skin (salt draws and then reabsorbs moisture).

**Turkey (breast)**
- - What salt does: same as chicken but more important because turkey breast is lean — ==salt helps retain moisture and season deeply.==
- - Practical: brine or salt several days ahead (longer diffusion time) for best juiciness. Pull from oven slightly before target temp because of carryover.

**Beef (steak)**
- - What salt does: seasons deeply (if salted early), draws moisture briefly to the surface then reabsorbs, helping both interior seasoning and promoting a better crust when surface is dry at sear time; salt also enhances Maillard browning perception by concentrating surface amino acids/sugars.
- - Practical: for thick steaks, salt 40+ minutes to several hours before cooking (or right before searing). Use coarse/kosher salt; if salting early, allow surface to dry for a better sear.

**Beef (tough cuts: brisket, shank)**
- - What salt does: seasons the meat and helps proteins and collagen interaction; when combined with long, moist, low heat (braise), salt helps flavors penetrate while collagen converts to gelatin.
- - Practical: salt before braising (can be early or just before) and cook low+long so salt distributes as collagen breaks down. Layer salty ingredients (stock, soy, cured elements) for depth.

- Pork (chops/loin)
- - What salt does: seasons and helps lean pork retain moisture (like chicken breast); improves flavor and juiciness.
- - Practical: salt ~30–60 minutes or up to several hours ahead. Pull at $60^\circ\text{C}$–$65^\circ\text{C}$ for juicier results. Use kosher salt and adjust if using finer table salt.

- Pork (shoulder)
- - What salt does: seasons through fat and connective tissue; helps render fat and, with slow cooking, contributes to gelatin formation so the meat becomes tender and juicy.
- - Practical: salt before long cook (dry rub or brine). Because shoulder is fatty/CT-rich, it tolerates and benefits from prolonged salting and slow, wet heat.

- Fish (fillet)
- - What salt does: seasons quickly, firms the flesh a bit (salt tightens and alters protein solubility), and can help skin crisp if surface is patted dry after salting.
- - Practical: salt shortly before cooking (minutes to up to an hour for thicker fillets). Avoid very long dry-brining for delicate fillets unless you want a firmer, slightly cured texture. Finish with a light flaky salt if desired.

- Shellfish (shrimp/prawns)
- - What salt does: seasons and enhances sweetness; small shellfish coagulate/firm quickly, so salt mainly affects flavor and surface texture.
- - Practical: season just before or during very brief cooking; remove immediately when done to avoid rubberiness.

- Eggs
- - What salt does: primarily flavors; salt also slightly affects protein coagulation (it can promote a more even set), so seasoned eggs often taste and set more pleasantly.
- - Practical: season eggs lightly before or after cooking depending on method. For poaching, a little salt in the poaching liquid seasons the white; for finished dishes use finishing salt.

- Beans (dried)
- - What salt does: seasons through as the starches gelatinize; salting the cooking water lets beans absorb seasoning while they soften. Salt also influences skin texture (evenness) while they cook.
- - Practical: simmer beans in salted water (salt early rather than only at the end). Use gentle simmering (not violent boil) so starches gelatinize and the beans become creamy.

Quick general rules you can apply to all ingredients
- - Salt unlocks aromatics, reduces bitterness, and balances sweetness — it makes ingredients “taste like themselves.”
- - For proteins, salt helps proteins solubilize into gels that hold water (more juiciness) and can help tenderness when combined with time (especially for collagen-rich cuts).
- - Timing matters: very early salting (dry-brine) lets salt diffuse through large cuts; immediate salting is fine for quick-cooking proteins or when you want a drier surface for searing.
- - Use kosher/Diamond Crystal for most seasoning (it’s less salty by volume than fine table salt); reserve flaky sea salts (Maldon, fleur de sel) for finishing.
