
### 1. Types of Salt

While chefs debate the merits of various salts, the book emphasizes using the right salt for the right task:

- **Table Salt:** Avoid if possible. It is very dense, often iodized (which adds a metallic taste), and contains anti-caking agents.

- **Kosher Salt (Diamond Crystal):** The author's preferred salt. It has light, hollow flakes that dissolve quickly and stick to food well. It is less salty by volume than table or Morton's salt.

- **Sea Salt:**
    
    - _Refined (Granular):_ Good for everyday cooking (pasta water, roasting).
    - _Flaky (Maldon, Fleur de Sel):_ Expensive and textural. Use only as a "finishing salt" to add crunch at the very end.

### 2. How to Use Salt (The "When" and "How")

- **Ditch the Shaker:** Use a bowl (salt cellar) and your fingers. This allows you to feel the amount you are using and distribute it evenly with a "wrist wag" rather than a robotic shake.
    
- **Season from Within:** Salt needs time to penetrate food.
    
    - _Meat:_ Salt early (a day in advance for chicken, days for turkey) to allow diffusion. This seasons the meat deep down and helps it retain moisture.
    - _Water:_ Salting water for boiling vegetables or pasta allows the food to absorb seasoning as it cooks. The water should taste "salty like the sea."
    -
- **Layering Salt:** Don't just rely on crystals. Incorporate salty ingredients like Parmesan, anchovies, soy sauce, bacon, or feta to build complex flavors.



## 4. Flavors and Texture

Salt in a marinade doesn't just add saltiness; it "unlocks" aromatic compounds in the other ingredients.

- It minimizes bitterness (e.g., from lemon juice or herbs).
- It balances sweetness (e.g., if you have sugar or honey in the marinade).
- It makes the meat taste more like itself (e.g., chicken tastes "more chickeny").
- **Juiciness:** Salt dissolves protein strands into a gel, allowing them to hold onto water better during cooking. This prevents the meat from drying out, keeping it juicy and tender.
- **Tenderness:** The salt helps break down tough muscle fibers, making the meat more tender to chew.
