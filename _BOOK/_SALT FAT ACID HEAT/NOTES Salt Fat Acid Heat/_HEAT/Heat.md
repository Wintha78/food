# How Heat Works

### Water and Heat

**Texture and Flavor Control**
*   **Moisture Management:** Cooking water out creates crispness or dryness (e.g., scrambled eggs); retaining or adding water ensures tenderness (e.g., rice, starches).
*   **Flavor Intensity:** Reduce water content (simmering/reducing) to concentrate flavors in soups and sauces. Conversely, excess water dilutes flavor (e.g., "watered down" stocks or overwatered produce).

**Freezing and Dehydration**
*   **Expansion:** Water expands when frozen. Leave headroom in containers to prevent bursting.
*   **Cellular Damage:** At the cellular level, expanding ice crystals burst cell walls. This causes "stalactites" (internal water escaping) and leathery textures (freezer burn).
*   **Best Foods to Freeze:** Choose items that withstand or require rehydration, such as braising cuts, stews, soups, and beans in liquid.

**Water as a Cooking Medium**
*   **Gentle Heat (Below 100°C):** Water baths, simmering, and poaching provide stable, low temperatures ideal for delicate custards or breaking down tough connective tissues.
*   **Boiling (100°C):** An efficient, rapid cooking method. 
*   **Safety:** Boiling acts as a built-in thermometer (visible bubbles) and kills pathogenic bacteria. Always bring leftovers or stocks to a boil when reheating to ensure safety.

### Steam Power

**The Temperature Barrier**
*   **100°C Limit:** As long as surface moisture is present and steaming, food cannot exceed 100°C.
*   **Browning Inhibition:** Steam prevents the Maillard reaction and caramelization, which require much higher temperatures.

**Moisture & Texture Control**
*   **Trapping Steam:** Use lids or pack food tightly to maintain a moist environment (~100°C). This wilts greens and "sweats" vegetables (cooking without color).
*   **Releasing Steam:** Spread food out anusd use sloped-sided pans to allow steam to escape. This is essential for browning, searing, and crisping.
*   **Internal Changes:** Steam replaces air in plant cells, turning vegetables from opaque to translucent while intensifying flavor and reducing volume.

**Tool & Technique Selection**
*   **Vessel Shape:** Tall, straight sides (pots) trap steam; sloped or shallow sides (pans) encourage evaporation.
*   **Packing Density:** 
    *   *Tight:* Protects dense vegetables (onions, artichokes) from burning while they cook through.
    *   *Spaced:* Allows quicker browning for high-moisture vegetables (courgettes, peppers).
*   **Salt Interaction:** 
    *   *To sweat:* Salt early to draw out moisture and create steam.
    *   *To brown:* Salt after crisping or far in advance (then pat dry) to prevent surface moisture from steaming the food.
## 1. Starch Transformation (Tenderizing)

Carbohydrates in the form of starches—found in potatoes, grains, dried beans, and root vegetables—are generally hard and indigestible in their raw state.

- **The Process:** Starches require **gentle heat and water** to transform. As they are heated in the presence of liquid, starch granules absorb the water and swell.
- **The Result:** This process, often called gelatinization, turns hard granules into soft, edible matter. For example, a hard, dried bean becomes creamy, and a grain of raw rice becomes fluffy and tender.
